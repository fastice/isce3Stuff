## 1. Background and Motivation

The ionosphere — a region of the upper atmosphere (60–1000 km altitude) permanently ionised by solar UV and X-ray radiation — is the dominant source of ranging error in L-band and lower-frequency SAR systems. Because the ionosphere is a **dispersive medium** (its refractive index depends on frequency), the radar carrier wave and the radar pulse envelope experience opposite-sign propagation effects: the carrier phase is **advanced** (the wave appears to travel faster than the speed of light), while the group (pulse envelope) is **delayed**.

For interferometric SAR, the effect manifests as an additional phase contribution in the interferogram that is **not due to surface displacement or topography** but is instead proportional to the differential Total Electron Content (ΔTEC) between the two acquisition times. This spurious phase can reach tens of centimetres of equivalent range displacement at L-band (1.26 GHz) under active ionospheric conditions — comparable to or exceeding the geophysical signals of interest.

ISCE3 addresses this through the **split-spectrum method (SSM)**, which uses the dispersive frequency-dependence of the ionospheric phase to separate it from the non-dispersive (tropospheric + deformation) component using only the data itself, without requiring external ionosphere models. External Global Ionosphere Map (GIM) corrections are also supported as an alternative or complement.

## 2. Ionospheric Physics

### 2.1 The Ionosphere as a Dispersive Medium

The ionosphere is a weakly ionised plasma. Its electromagnetic properties are characterised by the **plasma frequency**:

$$f_p = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{N_e\, e^2}{m_e\, \varepsilon_0}}$$

where:

| Symbol | Quantity | Value |
|--------|----------|-------|
| $N_e$ | Electron number density (m⁻³) | $10^{10}$–$10^{12}$ m⁻³ |
| $e$ | Electron charge | $1.602\times10^{-19}$ C |
| $m_e$ | Electron mass | $9.109\times10^{-31}$ kg |
| $\varepsilon_0$ | Permittivity of free space | $8.854\times10^{-12}$ F/m |

The plasma frequency evaluates to:

$$f_p \approx 9\sqrt{N_e}\quad\text{(Hz, with }N_e\text{ in m}^{-3}\text{)}$$

Typical peak $f_p$ in the F-layer is 4–12 MHz — well below the SAR carrier frequencies (L-band: 1.26 GHz, C-band: 5.4 GHz), which is why the simplest approximations apply.

### 2.2 Plasma Refractive Index

The Appleton-Hartree equation gives the full ionospheric refractive index. For transverse propagation and radar frequencies $f \gg f_p$, it reduces to the **quasi-longitudinal approximation**:

$$\boxed{n \approx 1 - \frac{f_p^2}{2f^2} = 1 - \frac{K\, N_e}{f^2}}$$

where:

$$K = \frac{e^2}{8\pi^2 m_e \varepsilon_0} = 40.308\;\text{m}^3\text{s}^{-2}$$

is the **ionospheric constant** (sometimes quoted as 40.3 or 40.28 in the literature due to rounding). The key result is that $n < 1$, making the ionosphere a **phase-advancing** medium for electromagnetic waves.

The **second-order correction** (important for geodetic-grade accuracy but typically ignored in SAR processing) is:

$$n \approx 1 - \frac{K N_e}{f^2} - \frac{K_2 N_e}{f^3}\cos\Theta + \mathcal{O}(f^{-4})$$

where $K_2$ depends on the geomagnetic field magnitude $B_0$ and $\Theta$ is the angle between the wave propagation direction and $B_0$.

### 2.3 Phase Velocity and Group Velocity

The phase velocity of the carrier wave and group velocity of the pulse envelope are:

$$v_\phi = \frac{c}{n} \approx c\!\left(1 + \frac{KN_e}{f^2}\right) > c$$

$$v_g = c\, n \approx c\!\left(1 - \frac{KN_e}{f^2}\right) < c$$

These are equal in magnitude of the deviation from $c$ but **opposite in sign**. This is a fundamental property of dispersive media and the physical basis for separating ionospheric from non-dispersive effects.

### 2.4 Total Electron Content (TEC)

The **Slant Total Electron Content** (STEC) along a propagation path $\ell$ is:

$$\text{STEC} = \int_\ell N_e\, d\ell \quad\text{[electrons/m}^2\text{]}$$

The standard unit is the **TECU** (TEC Unit):

$$1\;\text{TECU} = 10^{16}\;\text{electrons/m}^2$$

Typical values:

| Condition | VTEC (TECU) | L-band phase error |
|---|---|---|
| Night-time, mid-latitude | 1–5 | <1 cm |
| Day-time, mid-latitude | 10–30 | 3–8 cm |
| Equatorial electrojet | 30–80 | 8–21 cm |
| Solar maximum, equatorial | 50–150 | 13–40 cm |

### 2.5 Slant TEC and the Mapping Function

The **Vertical TEC** (VTEC) is related to STEC via the **Single-Layer Model (SLM)** mapping function:

$$\text{STEC} = M(\theta_z)\cdot\text{VTEC}$$

$$M(\theta_z) = \frac{1}{\cos\theta_z'} = \left[1 - \left(\frac{R_E \sin\theta_z}{R_E + H_{\text{ion}}}\right)^2\right]^{-1/2}$$

where:
- $\theta_z$ is the zenith angle at the ground station
- $\theta_z'$ is the zenith angle at the ionospheric shell height $H_{\text{ion}}$
- $R_E = 6371$ km is the mean Earth radius
- $H_{\text{ion}} = 450$ km is the standard single-layer shell height

The mapping function ranges from $M = 1$ at zenith to $M \approx 3$ at $\theta_z = 70°$. For SAR at typical incidence angles $\theta_i = 25°$–$50°$, $M \approx 1.1$–$1.4$.

### 2.6 Solar and Geomagnetic Dependence

TEC is driven by:

- **Solar EUV/X-ray flux** — increases with solar maximum (11-year cycle); local noon maximum, pre-dawn minimum
- **Geographic latitude** — equatorial ionisation anomaly (EIA) creates enhanced TEC at ±10–15° geomagnetic latitude
- **Season** — semi-annual and annual modulations
- **Geomagnetic storms** — the planetary geomagnetic index $K_p > 4$ indicates disturbed conditions; TEC can increase or decrease depending on phase of the storm and latitude
- **Scintillation** — rapid TEC fluctuations at equatorial and polar latitudes driven by plasma instabilities (Rayleigh-Taylor, gradient-drift)

## 3. Governing Equations for SAR

### 3.1 One-Way Phase Advance

The accumulated phase along a one-way path $\ell$ through the ionosphere, relative to free-space propagation, is:

$$\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}^{(1)} = \frac{2\pi f}{c}\int_\ell (n - 1)\, d\ell = \frac{2\pi f}{c}\int_\ell\!\left(-\frac{KN_e}{f^2}\right)d\ell = -\frac{2\pi K}{cf}\,\text{STEC}$$

The **negative sign** indicates phase advance (the carrier arrives earlier than it would in free space).

### 3.2 One-Way Group Delay

The one-way group delay along the same path is:

$$\Delta\tau_{\text{ion}}^{(1)} = \frac{1}{c}\int_\ell (n_g - 1)\,d\ell = \frac{1}{c}\int_\ell\!\left(\frac{KN_e}{f^2}\right)d\ell = +\frac{K}{cf^2}\,\text{STEC}$$

where $n_g = 1/v_g \cdot c \approx 1 + KN_e/f^2$ is the group refractive index. The **positive sign** means the pulse envelope is delayed relative to free-space travel.

The **equivalence** $|\Delta\phi^{(1)}| = 2\pi f |\Delta\tau^{(1)}|$ confirms the phase and group effects are equal in magnitude.

### 3.3 Two-Way Radar Phase Shift

SAR measures the two-way travel, so the ionospheric phase contribution to the focused image is twice the one-way phase advance:

$$\boxed{\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}} = -\frac{4\pi K}{c f}\,\text{STEC}}$$

For an interferometric pair with differential STEC $\Delta\text{STEC} = \text{STEC}_2 - \text{STEC}_1$ (secondary minus reference), the interferometric ionospheric phase is:

$$\boxed{\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}^{\text{ifg}} = -\frac{4\pi K}{c f}\,\Delta\text{STEC}}$$

Inserting numbers for L-band ($f = 1.2575\times10^9$ Hz):

$$\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}^{\text{L}} = -\frac{4\pi \times 40.308}{2.998\times10^8 \times 1.2575\times10^9}\;\Delta\text{STEC} \approx -\frac{1.345\times10^{-9}}{1}\;\Delta\text{STEC}\;\text{[rad per electron/m}^2\text{]}$$

For $\Delta\text{STEC} = 1\;\text{TECU} = 10^{16}$ electrons/m²:

$$\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}^{\text{L}} \approx -0.1345\;\text{rad} \approx -7.7°\;\text{per TECU}$$

### 3.4 Range Delay

The two-way ionospheric range delay (apparent range change, positive = increased apparent range) is:

$$\boxed{\Delta r_{\text{ion}} = \frac{c\,\Delta\tau_{\text{ion}}^{(2\text{-way})}}{2} = +\frac{K}{f^2}\,\Delta\text{STEC}}$$

For L-band at 1 TECU:

$$\Delta r_{\text{ion}}^{\text{L}} = \frac{40.308 \times 10^{16}}{(1.2575\times10^9)^2} \approx 0.0255\;\text{m}\;\text{per TECU}$$

Note the **sign convention**: the phase advance is negative (ionosphere shortens the apparent phase path), but the group delay is positive (ionosphere lengthens the apparent group path). These are related by:

$$\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}} = -\frac{4\pi f}{c}\Delta r_{\text{ion}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta r_{\text{ion}} = -\frac{c\,\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}}{4\pi f}$$

### 3.5 Dispersive vs Non-Dispersive Phase

The total interferometric phase at any pixel can be written as:

$$\phi_{\text{total}} = \phi_{\text{non-disp}} + \phi_{\text{disp}}(f)$$

where:

$$\phi_{\text{non-disp}} = \frac{4\pi f}{c}\Delta r_{\text{non-disp}} \propto f$$

$$\phi_{\text{disp}} = -\frac{4\pi K}{cf}\Delta\text{STEC} \propto f^{-1}$$

The non-dispersive component (surface deformation, troposphere, flat-earth, topography) scales **linearly with frequency**, while the ionospheric component scales **inversely with frequency**. This opposite frequency behaviour is exploited by the split-spectrum method.

### 3.6 Frequency Scaling Laws

From the governing equations, all ionospheric effects scale predictably with frequency:

| Effect | Scaling | L-band (1.26 GHz) | C-band (5.4 GHz) | X-band (9.6 GHz) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase shift $\Delta\phi$ | $\propto f^{-1}$ | $-7.7°$/TECU | $-1.8°$/TECU | $-1.0°$/TECU |
| Range delay $\Delta r$ | $\propto f^{-2}$ | $2.55$ cm/TECU | $1.38$ mm/TECU | $0.44$ mm/TECU |
| Faraday rotation $\Omega$ | $\propto f^{-2}$ | Significant | Negligible | Negligible |

At 50 TECU (severe conditions):
- L-band: ~**12.7 m** apparent range shift — correction **essential**
- C-band: ~**6.9 cm** apparent range shift — correction **recommended**
- X-band: ~**2.2 cm** apparent range shift — correction **optional**

## 4. The Split-Spectrum Method

### 4.1 Principle

The split-spectrum method (Brcic et al. 2010, Gomba et al. 2016) divides the radar bandwidth into two sub-bands centred at different frequencies. Because ionospheric phase scales as $f^{-1}$ while non-dispersive phase scales as $f^{+1}$, computing interferograms at both sub-band frequencies gives two equations in two unknowns ($\phi_{\text{non-disp}}$ and $\phi_{\text{disp}}$), which can be solved algebraically.

### 4.2 Sub-Band Phase Model

Let the full-band SLC be centred at frequency $f_0$ with bandwidth $B$. ISCE3 produces two sub-band SLCs:

- **Low sub-band:** centre frequency $f_L = f_0 - \Delta f/2$, bandwidth $B_{\text{sub}}$
- **High sub-band:** centre frequency $f_H = f_0 + \Delta f/2$, bandwidth $B_{\text{sub}}$

where $\Delta f$ is the frequency separation between sub-band centres and $B_{\text{sub}} < B/2$.

The interferometric phase in each sub-band (ignoring noise) is:

$$\phi_L = \phi_{\text{non-disp}}\frac{f_L}{f_0} + \phi_{\text{disp}}\frac{f_0}{f_L}$$

$$\phi_H = \phi_{\text{non-disp}}\frac{f_H}{f_0} + \phi_{\text{disp}}\frac{f_0}{f_H}$$

where $\phi_{\text{non-disp}}$ and $\phi_{\text{disp}}$ are defined at the reference frequency $f_0$.

More explicitly, substituting the governing equations:

$$\phi_L = \frac{4\pi f_L}{c}\Delta r_{\text{non-disp}} - \frac{4\pi K}{c f_L}\Delta\text{STEC}$$

$$\phi_H = \frac{4\pi f_H}{c}\Delta r_{\text{non-disp}} - \frac{4\pi K}{c f_H}\Delta\text{STEC}$$

### 4.3 TEC Estimation from Sub-Band Difference

Forming the difference $\phi_H - \phi_L$ eliminates the non-dispersive term only if the non-dispersive phase is frequency-independent — which is exact for deformation and flat-earth phase, and approximately true for tropospheric delay (to the accuracy of the non-dispersive approximation):

$$\phi_H - \phi_L = \frac{4\pi\Delta r_{\text{non-disp}}}{c}(f_H - f_L) - \frac{4\pi K\,\Delta\text{STEC}}{c}\!\left(\frac{1}{f_H} - \frac{1}{f_L}\right)$$

Solving for $\Delta\text{STEC}$:

$$\boxed{\Delta\text{STEC} = \frac{\phi_H - \phi_L - \frac{4\pi\Delta f}{c}\Delta r_{\text{non-disp}}}{-\frac{4\pi K}{c}\!\left(\frac{1}{f_H}-\frac{1}{f_L}\right)}}$$

Since the non-dispersive contribution to $\phi_H - \phi_L$ is typically small (it scales as $\Delta f / f_0 \ll 1$ relative to the full-band phase), the **practical estimator** used in ISCE3 is:

$$\boxed{\widehat{\Delta\text{STEC}} = \frac{f_L f_H}{4\pi K (f_H - f_L)/c}\cdot\frac{f_H \phi_L - f_L \phi_H}{f_H^2 - f_L^2} \times \frac{c}{-4\pi K}}$$

Or, in a more compact form commonly cited in the literature:

$$\widehat{\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}} = \frac{f_L^2\,\phi_H - f_H^2\,\phi_L}{f_L^2 - f_H^2}$$

This is the **ionospheric phase estimate at the reference frequency $f_0$**. The non-dispersive estimate is:

$$\widehat{\phi_{\text{non-disp}}} = \frac{f_H^2\,\phi_H - f_L^2\,\phi_L}{f_H^2 - f_L^2}$$

It is straightforward to verify that $\widehat{\phi_{\text{ion}}} + \widehat{\phi_{\text{non-disp}}} \equiv \phi_{\text{full-band}}$.

### 4.4 Full-Band Interferogram Correction

Given the ionospheric phase screen estimate $\widehat{\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}}(x, y)$ at each pixel, the corrected full-band interferogram is:

$$\phi_{\text{corrected}}(x, y) = \phi_{\text{full-band}}(x, y) - \widehat{\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}}(x, y)$$

In complex form (preserving coherence structure):

$$I_{\text{corrected}}(x, y) = I_{\text{full-band}}(x, y)\cdot\exp\!\left(-j\,\widehat{\Delta\phi_{\text{ion}}}(x,y)\right)$$

### 4.5 Error Sources and Sensitivity

**Phase noise amplification.** Because the estimator divides a phase difference by the small factor $(f_H - f_L)/f_0$, noise in the sub-band phases is amplified. The noise amplification factor is:

$$A_{\text{noise}} = \frac{f_0}{\Delta f} = \frac{f_0}{f_H - f_L}$$

For NISAR L-band with $f_0 = 1.2575$ GHz and $\Delta f = 20$ MHz, $A_{\text{noise}} \approx 63$. This means sub-band coherence must be substantially higher than full-band coherence to yield a usable TEC estimate.

**Phase unwrapping errors.** Sub-band interferograms must be unwrapped before the difference is computed. Unwrapping errors introduce 2π jumps in the estimated ionospheric phase, which manifest as localized phase artefacts in the correction.

**Wavenumber shift decorrelation.** Different sub-band centre frequencies imply different effective wavenumbers, causing a decorrelation similar to perpendicular baseline decorrelation in the range direction. The critical bandwidth (analogous to critical baseline) is:

$$B_{\text{crit}} \approx \frac{B}{\tan\theta_i}\cdot\frac{f_0}{B_{\text{sub}}}$$

**Non-dispersive residual.** Wideband tropospheric dispersion introduces a small residual in the non-dispersive estimate. For typical zenith wet delays, this residual is < 0.5 mm at L-band.

**Polarimetric phase bias (NISAR QQP).** As detailed in Section 7.3, the use of different polarisations in the two sub-bands introduces a polarimetric phase offset that masquerades as ionospheric signal.

**Sensitivity to $\Delta f$:**

$$\sigma_{\Delta\text{STEC}} = \frac{A_{\text{noise}} \cdot \sigma_\phi}{4\pi K/cf_0^2} = \frac{f_0^2 \sigma_\phi c}{\Delta f \cdot 4\pi K}$$

Larger $\Delta f$ reduces noise; larger bandwidth $B_{\text{sub}}$ (lower $\sigma_\phi$) also reduces noise.

## 5. Range vs Azimuth Split-Spectrum

The original split-spectrum method divides the **range bandwidth**. An alternative is to divide the **azimuth (Doppler) bandwidth**. Both are supported in principle; ISCE3 primarily implements the range split-spectrum.

| Criterion | Range split-spectrum | Azimuth split-spectrum |
|---|---|---|
| **What is split** | Range (fast-time) bandwidth $B$ | Azimuth (Doppler) bandwidth $B_D$ |
| **Physical basis** | Ionospheric dispersion in range | Different viewing angles → different path lengths through ionosphere |
| **TEC sensitivity** | Depends on $\Delta f$ and $f_0$ | Depends on baseline, usually lower |
| **Applicability** | Any SAR mode | Requires sufficient squint diversity or azimuth oversampling |
| **Coherence requirement** | Moderate (range coherence) | Low (azimuth coherence degrades at long baselines) |
| **ISCE3 default** | **Yes** | Experimental / not in main workflow |
| **NISAR** | Primary method | Secondary validation |
| **Azimuth streak removal** | No | Can capture azimuth-varying TEC |

### 6.2 Sub-Band Filtering

The `SplitSpectrum` class applies a **bandpass filter** in the range frequency domain to extract each sub-band. The procedure for each SLC is:

1. **FFT** each range line: $S(\omega) = \mathcal{F}\{s(\tau)\}$
2. **Apply bandpass window** centred on $\omega_L$ or $\omega_H$ with width $B_{\text{sub}}$:

$$H_{\text{sub}}(\omega) = W\!\left(\frac{\omega - \omega_{\text{sub}}}{2\pi B_{\text{sub}}}\right)$$

where $W$ is a spectral window (Hamming, Blackman, or rectangular) that determines the trade-off between frequency selectivity and sidelobe suppression.

3. **Inverse FFT**: $s_{\text{sub}}(\tau) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{S(\omega)\cdot H_{\text{sub}}(\omega)\}$

4. **Demodulate** to baseband at $f_{\text{sub}}$:

$$s_{\text{sub,bb}}(\tau) = s_{\text{sub}}(\tau)\cdot\exp\!\left(-j2\pi(f_{\text{sub}} - f_0)\tau\right)$$

This produces two sub-band SLCs, each at a lower effective bandwidth $B_{\text{sub}}$ but retaining the full azimuth resolution.

```python
import isce3

split = isce3.signal.SplitSpectrum()

# Extract sub-bands from a full-bandwidth SLC raster
split.split_spectrum(
    slc_raster        = slc_raster,          # isce3.io.Raster, complex64
    low_sub_raster    = low_band_raster,     # output: low sub-band SLC
    high_sub_raster   = high_band_raster,    # output: high sub-band SLC
    range_bandwidth   = 80.0e6,              # full range bandwidth (Hz)
    center_frequency  = 1.2575e9,            # carrier frequency (Hz)
    low_sub_bw        = 20.0e6,              # low sub-band bandwidth (Hz)
    high_sub_bw       = 20.0e6,             # high sub-band bandwidth (Hz)
    window_function   = "cosine",            # spectral window type
    window_parameter  = 0.0                  # window shape parameter
)
```

### 6.3 Sub-Band Interferogram Formation

After extracting sub-band SLCs from both the reference and secondary acquisitions, sub-band interferograms are formed using the standard cross-multiplication (crossmul) step:

$$I_{\text{sub}}(x, y) = s_{\text{ref,sub}}(x,y)\cdot s_{\text{sec,sub}}^*(x,y)$$

This is done for both the low and high sub-bands:

```python
# Cross-multiply low sub-band
crossmul = isce3.signal.Crossmul()
crossmul.crossmul(
    referenceSLC  = ref_low_raster,
    secondarySLC  = sec_low_raster,
    interferogram = ifgram_low_raster,
    coherence     = coh_low_raster,
    rangeLooks    = rg_looks,
    azimuthLooks  = az_looks
)

# Repeat for high sub-band → ifgram_high_raster, coh_high_raster
```

The sub-band interferograms are then **phase-unwrapped** using ICU or SNAPHU (same algorithms as the full-band interferogram — see main user manual).

### 6.4 TEC Estimation Pipeline

The complete TEC estimation pipeline in ISCE3 proceeds as:

```
ref_SLC ──┬──► low sub-band SLC ──┐
          │                        ├──► crossmul ──► φ_low (wrapped)
sec_SLC ──┤──► low sub-band SLC ──┘      │
          │                              ▼
          │                         unwrap (ICU/SNAPHU)
          │                              │
          │                              ▼ φ_low (unwrapped)
          │                                           │
          ├──► high sub-band SLC ──┐                  │
          │                        ├──► crossmul ──► φ_high (wrapped)
          └──► high sub-band SLC ──┘      │
                                          ▼
                                     unwrap (ICU/SNAPHU)
                                          │
                                          ▼ φ_high (unwrapped)
                                          │
                             ┌────────────┴────────────┐
                             │   TEC estimation:        │
                             │   φ_ion = (fL²φH-fH²φL) │
                             │           /(fL²-fH²)     │
                             └────────────┬────────────┘
                                          │
                                    spatial filter
                                          │
                                     φ_ion screen
```

```python
import isce3.ionosphere as ion

# Estimate ionospheric phase from unwrapped sub-band interferograms
ion_phase = ion.estimate_ionosphere(
    phi_low          = unwrapped_low_raster,   # isce3.io.Raster
    phi_high         = unwrapped_high_raster,  # isce3.io.Raster
    freq_low         = f_L,                    # Hz
    freq_high        = f_H,                    # Hz
    coherence_low    = coh_low_raster,
    coherence_high   = coh_high_raster,
    coherence_thresh = 0.5
)
```

### 6.5 Spatial Filtering of the Ionospheric Phase Screen

The raw TEC estimate is noisy due to the amplification factor $A_{\text{noise}} = f_0 / \Delta f$. ISCE3 applies a two-step spatial filtering approach:

**Step 1 — Outlier removal.** Pixels with sub-band coherence below a threshold (default 0.5) are masked. Remaining outliers are identified using the **spatial fluctuation of TEC (SFT)** metric — the local standard deviation of the TEC estimate in a sliding window. Pixels where SFT exceeds a threshold are replaced by spatial interpolation.

**Step 2 — Adaptive low-pass filter.** A coherence-weighted Gaussian (or median) filter suppresses residual high-frequency noise:

$$\hat{\phi}_{\text{ion}}^{\text{filt}}(x,y) = \frac{\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{W}} \gamma(i,j)\,\hat{\phi}_{\text{ion}}(i,j) \cdot g(x-i,\, y-j)}{\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{W}} \gamma(i,j)\,g(x-i,\,y-j)}$$

where $g(x,y) = \exp(-(x^2+y^2)/(2\sigma^2))$ is a Gaussian kernel and $\gamma(i,j)$ is the sub-band coherence weight. The filter kernel size $\sigma$ is set adaptively based on the estimated noise level.

Default filter parameters in ISCE3:

| Parameter | Default |
|---|---|
| Phase filter kernel (max) | 200 pixels |
| Phase filter kernel (min) | 100 pixels |
| Azimuth shift filter kernel (max) | 150 pixels |
| Azimuth shift filter kernel (min) | 75 pixels |
| Coherence threshold | 0.5 |
| Filter type | Gaussian (median optional) |

### 6.6 Correction Application

The filtered ionospheric phase screen is applied to the full-band unwrapped interferogram:

```python
import isce3.ionosphere as ion

ion.apply_ionosphere_correction(
    interferogram      = unwrapped_full_raster,   # input: full-band unwrapped
    ionosphere_screen  = ion_phase_screen,         # estimated φ_ion
    corrected_ifg      = corrected_raster,         # output
    sign               = -1                        # subtract ionospheric phase
)
```

In the NISAR workflow, the ionospheric phase screen is **not applied** to the GUNW product by default. Instead, it is written as a **separate layer** alongside the interferogram, allowing users to apply or ignore it depending on their scientific application.

## 7. NISAR-Specific Implementation

### 7.1 Dual-Band Architecture

NISAR operates simultaneously in two radar bands:

| Band | Centre Frequency | Wavelength | Ionospheric sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| L-band | $f_L = 1.2575$ GHz | $\lambda_L = 23.8$ cm | High ($\propto f^{-2}$) |
| S-band | $f_S = 3.19$ GHz | $\lambda_S = 9.4$ cm | $\sim 6\times$ lower than L-band |

The L/S dual-frequency architecture provides an **independent** ionospheric correction path: the differential phase between the L-band and S-band interferograms can be used to estimate ionospheric TEC directly, analogous to dual-frequency GNSS:

$$\phi_{L} - \phi_{S}\!\left(\frac{f_L}{f_S}\right) = -\frac{4\pi K}{c}\Delta\text{STEC}\!\left(\frac{1}{f_L} - \frac{1}{f_S}\right)$$

However, this requires careful handling of the different spatial resolutions and coverage areas of the two bands, and is more complex than the single-band split-spectrum method.

### 7.2 L-Band Split-Spectrum Configuration

For NISAR L-band split-spectrum, the bandwidth allocation is:

```
|←————————— L-band total bandwidth ~80 MHz —————————→|
|   Main band (20 MHz)    |  Guard  |  Side band (5 MHz)  |
|  f_L = f₀ - 37.5 MHz   |         |  f_H = f₀ + 37.5 MHz |
```

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Carrier frequency $f_0$ | 1.2575 GHz |
| Main sub-band bandwidth $B_L$ | 20 MHz |
| Side sub-band bandwidth $B_H$ | 5 MHz |
| Sub-band centre separation $\Delta f$ | ~75 MHz |
| Noise amplification factor $A_{\text{noise}}$ | $f_0/\Delta f \approx 16.8$ |

The asymmetric bandwidths (20 MHz vs 5 MHz) mean the low sub-band has higher coherence and lower phase noise. The high sub-band's narrower bandwidth gives lower range resolution but this is acceptable for the slowly varying ionospheric signal.

### 7.3 Quasi-Quad Polarimetry (QQP) Polarimetric Phase Bias

NISAR operates in Quasi-Quad Polarimetry (QQP) mode in which:
- Main band transmits **H** polarisation → receives HH and HV
- Side band transmits **V** polarisation → receives VH and VV

This means the main and side sub-bands use different polarisations. The **polarimetric phase difference** between HH and VV (or cross-pol combinations) introduces a bias in the ionospheric phase estimate that is **not ionospheric** in origin:

$$\hat{\phi}_{\text{ion}} = \phi_{\text{ion,true}} + \underbrace{\Delta\phi_{\text{pol}}}_{\text{bias}}$$

where $\Delta\phi_{\text{pol}}$ is the HH-VV scattering phase difference, which can be:

- Up to 30° over 12-day repeat periods for bare soil and forest
- Up to 90° for heavily vegetated areas
- Dependent on soil moisture, canopy structure, and incidence angle

**Mitigation strategies:**

1. **Use same-polarisation products** — only HH-HH or VV-VV for the split-spectrum, not cross-polarisation combinations. This requires extracting like-polarisation data from the QQP data streams.

2. **Apply polarimetric correction** — estimate $\Delta\phi_{\text{pol}}$ from co-pol ratio and apply a correction prior to ionospheric estimation.

3. **Filter temporally** — over long time series, the polarimetric phase bias is relatively stable compared to the ionospheric phase, allowing separation via temporal filtering (MintPy integration).

### 7.4 Ionospheric Phase in NISAR L2 Products

The ionospheric phase screen appears in NISAR L2 products as follows:

| Product | Ionosphere handling |
|---|---|
| RIFG (wrapped interferogram) | No ionosphere correction applied |
| RUNW (unwrapped, range-Doppler) | Ionospheric phase screen included as separate layer; correction **not** applied |
| GUNW (unwrapped, geocoded) | Ionospheric phase screen geocoded to 80 m posting; correction **not** applied by default |

The GUNW HDF5 structure for the ionospheric layer:

```
/science/LSAR/GUNW/
  ionosphere/
    ionospherePhaseScreen           # estimated φ_ion (rad), float32
    ionospherePhaseScreenUncertainty# 1-sigma uncertainty (rad), float32
    ionospherePhaseScreenCoherence  # sub-band coherence, float32
```

Users wishing to apply the ionospheric correction subtract the phase screen from the unwrapped phase:

```python
import h5py
import numpy as np

with h5py.File("NISAR_GUNW.h5", "r") as f:
    phi_unw = f["/science/LSAR/GUNW/unwrappedPhase"][:]
    phi_ion = f["/science/LSAR/GUNW/ionosphere/ionospherePhaseScreen"][:]
    uncertainty = f["/science/LSAR/GUNW/ionosphere/ionospherePhaseScreenUncertainty"][:]

# Apply correction (subtract ionospheric phase)
phi_corrected = phi_unw - phi_ion

# Convert to LOS displacement (metres)
wavelength = 0.2385   # L-band, metres
los_displacement = phi_corrected * wavelength / (4 * np.pi)
```

## 8. External Ionosphere Models (GIM / IONEX)

### 8.1 IONEX Format

Global Ionosphere Maps (GIMs) are distributed in the **IONEX** (IONosphere Map EXchange) format, an ASCII standard maintained by the International GNSS Service (IGS).

A GIM provides **VTEC** on a regular geographic grid with the following default resolution:

| Parameter | Standard IGS GIM |
|---|---|
| Spatial resolution | 2.5° latitude × 5.0° longitude |
| Temporal resolution | 2 hours (some providers: 1 hour) |
| Shell height | 450 km |
| VTEC units | 0.1 TECU (stored as integer × 0.1) |
| Latency | Final: 2 weeks; Rapid: 1 day; Real-time: available |

Major IGS Analysis Centres providing GIMs:

| Centre | Code | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Center for Orbit Determination, Europe | CODE | IONEX standard |
| Jet Propulsion Laboratory | JPL | Also provides IMAGEN for NISAR |
| European Space Agency / ESOC | ESA | |
| Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya | UPC | TOMION model |
| Chinese Academy of Sciences | CAS | |
| Wuhan University | WHU | |

### 8.2 TEC Grid Interpolation

To obtain the STEC along a SAR signal path, the VTEC is first interpolated in space and time, then converted to STEC via the mapping function.

**Bilinear spatial interpolation** of VTEC at the ionospheric pierce point (IPP) coordinates $(\phi_{\text{IPP}}, \lambda_{\text{IPP}})$:

$$\text{VTEC}(\phi_{\text{IPP}}, \lambda_{\text{IPP}}, t) = \sum_{i,j\in\{0,1\}} w_i^{\phi}\, w_j^{\lambda}\, \text{VTEC}(\phi_i, \lambda_j, t)$$

where $w_0^\phi = 1 - (\phi_{\text{IPP}} - \phi_0)/\Delta\phi$, $w_1^\phi = (\phi_{\text{IPP}} - \phi_0)/\Delta\phi$, etc.

**Temporal interpolation** between maps at times $t_k$ and $t_{k+1}$:

$$\text{VTEC}(t) = (1-\alpha)\,\text{VTEC}(t_k) + \alpha\,\text{VTEC}(t_{k+1}),\quad \alpha = \frac{t - t_k}{t_{k+1} - t_k}$$

**IPP coordinates** for a SAR pixel at ground coordinates $(\phi_g, \lambda_g)$ viewed from platform position $(\phi_s, \lambda_s, h_s)$ at incidence angle $\theta_i$:

$$\phi_{\text{IPP}} = \phi_g + \Delta\phi_{\text{IPP}},\quad \lambda_{\text{IPP}} = \lambda_g + \Delta\lambda_{\text{IPP}}$$

where $\Delta\phi_{\text{IPP}}$ and $\Delta\lambda_{\text{IPP}}$ are the angular offsets from the surface point to the point where the line of sight pierces the ionospheric shell at $H_{\text{ion}} = 450$ km. The SLM mapping function (Section 2.5) converts VTEC to STEC at this IPP.

### 8.3 Applying a GIM Correction to InSAR

The GIM-based correction to an InSAR pair (acquisitions at times $t_1$ and $t_2$) is:

$$\Delta\phi_{\text{GIM}}(x, y) = -\frac{4\pi K}{c f_0}\!\left[\text{STEC}(x,y,t_2) - \text{STEC}(x,y,t_1)\right]$$

In ISCE3 the GIM correction path is configured via the `ionosphere_phase_correction` runconfig block (Section 13). There is no standalone public Python function for GIM correction analogous to the split-spectrum classes; the integration is handled inside the NISAR InSAR workflow (`nisar.workflows.ionosphere`). To apply a GIM correction, set `spectral_diversity: main_side_band` or leave the split-spectrum method active and additionally supply an external IONEX file via the `dynamic_ancillary_file_group` runconfig section. The workflow will then compute the STEC at each ionospheric pierce point, map it through the single-layer mapping function, and form the correction phase $\Delta\phi_{\text{GIM}}$.

### 8.4 Accuracy: Model-Based vs Data-Driven

| Method | Typical accuracy (TECU) | Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| Split-spectrum (ISCE3) | 1–3 TECU | Sufficient coherence, moderate ionosphere |
| GIM (CODE/JPL) | 2–5 TECU | Global, any weather |
| GIM (rapid product) | 3–7 TECU | Available within 24 hours |
| GIM (real-time) | 5–10 TECU | Available immediately |
| GNSS-based local correction | 0.5–2 TECU | Requires dense GNSS network nearby |

For L-band at 1 TECU accuracy:
$\sigma_{\Delta r} = K \sigma_{\text{STEC}} / f^2 \approx 0.025$ m per TECU → **2.5 cm** per TECU range error.

The split-spectrum method outperforms GIM-based methods when scene coherence is adequate ($\gamma_{\text{sub}} > 0.4$). GIM is preferred for very low-coherence scenes (e.g., forests over 12-day baselines) where the split-spectrum TEC estimate is too noisy.

## 9. Faraday Rotation

### 9.1 Physical Origin

The geomagnetic field $\mathbf{B}_0$ causes the ionospheric plasma to become **anisotropic** — it behaves differently for left-hand and right-hand circularly polarised (LHCP and RHCP) waves, which propagate with slightly different phase velocities. A linearly polarised wave (like the H or V transmit of a SAR) can be decomposed into LHCP + RHCP components; as they propagate through the ionosphere at different speeds, they accumulate a phase difference, causing the polarisation plane to rotate.

### 9.2 Governing Equations

The **Faraday rotation angle** $\Omega$ accumulated along the two-way path is:

$$\boxed{\Omega = \frac{e^3}{8\pi^2 \varepsilon_0 m_e^2 c\, f^2} \int_\ell N_e\,|\mathbf{B}_0\cdot\hat{\mathbf{k}}|\, d\ell}$$

This is often written as:

$$\Omega = \frac{K_F \cdot \text{RM}}{f^2}$$

where $K_F = 2.65\times10^{-3}$ rad·m²/T·TECU and the **Rotation Measure** RM is:

$$\text{RM} = \int_\ell N_e\, B_{0,\parallel}\, d\ell \quad\text{[T·electrons/m}^2\text{]}$$

with $B_{0,\parallel} = |\mathbf{B}_0\cdot\hat{\mathbf{k}}|$ the component of the geomagnetic field along the line of sight.

**Typical values at L-band:**

| Geomagnetic latitude | $\Omega$ (degrees) |
|---|---|
| High latitudes (polar) | 5–25° |
| Mid-latitudes | 1–10° |
| Equatorial | 1–5° |
| Solar maximum | Up to 2× baseline values |

Faraday rotation is **negligible** at C-band ($\propto f^{-2}$) and X-band.

### 9.3 Effect on Quad-Pol SAR and Correction

For a fully polarimetric (quad-pol) SAR, the observed polarimetric scattering matrix $\mathbf{M}$ is related to the true matrix $\mathbf{S}$ by:

$$\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{F}(\Omega)\,\mathbf{S}\,\mathbf{F}(\Omega)$$

where $\mathbf{F}(\Omega) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\Omega & -\sin\Omega\\ \sin\Omega & \cos\Omega\end{pmatrix}$ is the Faraday rotation matrix.

This causes:

- **HH-VV phase corruption**: Faraday rotation introduces a spurious HH-VV phase difference of $\sim 2\Omega$
- **Cross-talk**: Energy leaks between co- and cross-polarisation channels
- **Polarimetric decomposition errors**: Cloude-Pottier, Freeman-Durden, etc. produce biased results

**Estimating $\Omega$ from quad-pol data:**

Using the circular polarisation ratio (CPR) from the coherency matrix $\mathbf{T}$:

$$\tan(4\Omega) = \frac{2\,\text{Im}(T_{12} + T_{12}^*)}{T_{11} - T_{22} + 2T_{33}}$$

Or from the Hermitian asymmetry of the cross-pol terms:

$$\Omega \approx \frac{1}{4}\arg\!\left(\langle M_{HH} M_{VV}^*\rangle\right) - \frac{\phi_{HH-VV}}{4}$$

**Correction:**

$$\hat{\mathbf{S}} = \mathbf{F}^{-1}(-\Omega)\,\mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{F}^{-1}(-\Omega)$$

For single-pol or dual-pol SAR (HH or VV only), Faraday rotation introduces a uniform phase offset that cancels in differential interferometry if it is constant between acquisitions.

## 10. Azimuth Streaks and Ionospheric Scintillation

At equatorial and polar latitudes, the ionosphere contains **plasma irregularities** — localised structures of strongly enhanced or depleted electron density. These field-aligned irregularities (FAIs) are oriented along Earth's magnetic field lines, which at equatorial latitudes are approximately horizontal and roughly north-south.

A SAR signal propagating through FAIs experiences rapid TEC variations along its path. Because in stripmap SAR the imaging time for a given pixel spans many seconds (the synthetic aperture duration $T_{SA}$), azimuth-dependent phase variations across the aperture cause **azimuth defocusing** and characteristic linear artefacts aligned roughly in the azimuth direction — "ionospheric azimuth streaks".

The phase along the synthetic aperture due to a sinusoidal TEC perturbation $\delta\text{TEC}(\eta) = A\cos(2\pi\eta/P)$ is:

$$\delta\phi_{\text{ion}}(\eta) = -\frac{4\pi K}{cf_0}\cdot A\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi\eta}{P}\right)$$

This acts as an additional azimuth phase history superimposed on the target's Doppler phase, leading to:

- **Defocusing** if $P \sim T_{SA}$ (the perturbation period matches the aperture duration)
- **Displacement** in azimuth position if $P \gg T_{SA}$
- **Ghost targets** if $P < T_{SA}$

The **azimuth displacement** due to a linear TEC gradient $\dot{N} = d\text{TEC}/d\eta$ is:

$$\Delta x_{\text{az}} = \frac{\lambda R_0}{2v_s}\cdot\frac{d\phi_{\text{ion}}}{d\eta} = \frac{\lambda R_0}{2v_s}\cdot\frac{-4\pi K\dot{N}}{cf_0}$$

**Detection and mitigation in ISCE3:**

- Azimuth streaks appear as coherent linear artefacts in the interferogram perpendicular to range
- The spatial filtering step (Section 6.5) with an adaptive kernel in the azimuth direction can suppress high-spatial-frequency ionospheric contributions
- For severe scintillation, no current automated mitigation is fully effective; user masking of affected areas is recommended

### 12.1 SplitSpectrum Class

**`isce3.splitspectrum.SplitSpectrum`**

Primary class for sub-band extraction from a full-bandwidth SLC. Constructed with the SLC's sampling metadata, then called to bandpass and demodulate each range line.

```python
los_m = phi_corrected * wavelength / (4 * np.pi)

# ── Step 2: Form sub-band interferograms ──────────────────────────────────────
crossmul = isce3.signal.Crossmul()

ifgram_low = isce3.io.Raster("ifgram_low.int", ...)
coh_low    = isce3.io.Raster("coh_low.cor", ...)
crossmul.crossmul(ref_low, sec_low, ifgram_low, coh_low, rangeLooks=rg_looks, azimuthLooks=az_looks)

ifgram_high = isce3.io.Raster("ifgram_high.int", ...)
coh_high    = isce3.io.Raster("coh_high.cor", ...)
crossmul.crossmul(ref_high, sec_high, ifgram_high, coh_high, rangeLooks=rg_looks, azimuthLooks=az_looks)

# ── Step 3: Unwrap sub-band interferograms ────────────────────────────────────
icu = isce3.unwrap.ICU(buffer_lines=3700, overlap=200)

unw_low = isce3.io.Raster("unw_low.unw", ...)
icu.unwrap(ifgram_low, coh_low, unw_low, coherence_threshold=0.1)

unw_high = isce3.io.Raster("unw_high.unw", ...)
icu.unwrap(ifgram_high, coh_high, unw_high, coherence_threshold=0.1)

# ── Step 4: Estimate ionospheric phase ───────────────────────────────────────
ion_phase_raw = isce3.io.Raster("ion_phase_raw.ion", ...)
ion.estimate_ionosphere(
    phi_low=unw_low, phi_high=unw_high,
    freq_low=f_L, freq_high=f_H,
    coherence_low=coh_low, coherence_high=coh_high,
    output_raster=ion_phase_raw,
    coherence_thresh=0.5,
    ref_frequency=fc
)

# ── Step 5: Filter ionospheric phase screen ───────────────────────────────────
# Use average coherence as weight
coh_avg = isce3.io.Raster("coh_avg.cor", ...)
# ... (average coh_low and coh_high) ...

ion_phase_filt = isce3.io.Raster("ion_phase_filt.ion", ...)
ion.filter_ionosphere(
    ion_phase=ion_phase_raw,
    coherence=coh_avg,
    filtered_phase=ion_phase_filt,
    filter_type="gaussian",
    kernel_size_max=200,
    kernel_size_min=100,
    coherence_thresh=0.5
)

# ── Step 6: Apply correction to full-band unwrapped interferogram ─────────────
# (Assumes full-band unw_full already produced by main InSAR workflow)
unw_full      = isce3.io.Raster("unwrapped_full.unw")
unw_corrected = isce3.io.Raster("unwrapped_corrected.unw", ...)

ion.apply_ionosphere_correction(
    interferogram=unw_full,
    ionosphere_screen=ion_phase_filt,
    corrected_ifg=unw_corrected,
    sign=-1   # subtract ionospheric phase
)

print("Ionosphere correction complete.")
print(f"Noise amplification factor: {fc / (f_H - f_L):.1f}×")
print(f"Phase sensitivity: {4*3.14159*40.308e16/(3e8*fc):.4f} rad/TECU")
```

## 13. Runconfig YAML Reference

The ionosphere correction stage is configured within the InSAR workflow runconfig. Below is the full ionosphere section with all parameters:

```yaml
runconfig:
  groups:
    processing:
      ionosphere_phase_correction:
        # Enable/disable ionosphere correction
        enabled: true

        # Correction method
        spectral_diversity: split_main_band  # split_main_band | gim | disabled

        # Sub-band filter parameters
        split_range_spectrum:
          # Spectral window for sub-band extraction
          window_function: cosine    # cosine | hanning | rect | kaiser
          window_parameter: 0.0     # kaiser beta (0 → rectangular)

        # Phase unwrapping for sub-band interferograms
        unwrap_method: icu           # icu | snaphu
        unwrap_snaphu:
          cost_mode: SMOOTH

        # Spatial filtering of ionospheric phase screen
        phase_filter:
          filter_type: gaussian      # gaussian | median
          # Adaptive kernel size range (in pixels)
          kernel_range_max: 200
          kernel_range_min: 100
          # Azimuth shift filter kernel range
          kernel_az_max: 150
          kernel_az_min: 75

        # Coherence masking
        coherence_threshold: 0.5

        # Output options
        output_ionosphere_screen: true    # Write screen to product
        output_uncertainty: true          # Write 1σ uncertainty
        apply_correction: false           # Apply to GUNW (false = screen only)

        # GIM-based correction (alternative to split-spectrum)
        gim:
          enabled: false
          ionex_dir: /path/to/ionex/files/
          ion_height_km: 450.0

      # Frequency/polarization to use for ionosphere estimation
      # (relevant for NISAR QQP mode)
      ionosphere_frequency: A            # A | B
      ionosphere_polarization: co-pol    # co-pol | cross-pol
```

## 14. InSAR Workflow Integration

The ionosphere correction module is called as one stage of the full InSAR workflow. Its position in the pipeline is:

```
Reference RSLC ──┐
                 ├── Coregistration ──► Coregistered sec SLC
Secondary RSLC ──┘         │
                            ▼
                    Range/azimuth filtering
                            │
                            ▼
                    Full-band crossmul ──► RIFG (wrapped)
                            │
                            ▼
                    Coherence estimation
                            │
                     ┌──────┴──────────────────────┐
                     │  Ionosphere correction stage │◄── This module
                     │  (split-spectrum or GIM)     │
                     └──────────────────────────────┘
                            │ φ_ion screen
                            │
                    Full-band unwrapping ──► RUNW
                            │
                    Geocoding ──────────► GUNW + ion screen layer
```

The ionosphere correction stage is invoked from:

```bash
python -m nisar.workflows.insar \
  --run-config-path insar_runconfig.yaml
```

The stage can also be run **standalone** to re-estimate the ionospheric phase screen without re-running the full InSAR chain:

```bash
python -m nisar.workflows.ionosphere \
  --run-config-path ionosphere_runconfig.yaml \
  --input-hdf5 insar_product.h5 \
  --output-hdf5 insar_product_ion.h5
```

## 15. Troubleshooting and Common Pitfalls

### Ionospheric correction makes interferogram worse

- **Cause:** Sub-band coherence too low to estimate TEC reliably; noise is amplified into the correction.
- **Fix:** Raise `coherence_threshold` to 0.6–0.7. Increase `kernel_range_max` to apply stronger smoothing. If sub-band coherence is below ~0.3 across most of the scene, disable ionosphere correction entirely (`enabled: false`).

### Phase screen has stripy artefacts aligned in range

- **Cause:** Unwrapping errors in sub-band interferograms. These manifest as horizontal stripes (constant-range lines) where 2π jumps occurred.
- **Fix:** Check sub-band coherence maps. Increase multi-look factors (`rg_looks`, `az_looks`) for better coherence before unwrapping. Consider using SNAPHU instead of ICU for better handling of isolated low-coherence regions.

### Correction overcorrects (worse displacement map)

- **Cause 1:** QQP polarimetric phase bias (NISAR) — the HH-VV phase difference was interpreted as ionospheric.
- **Fix:** Set `ionosphere_polarization: co-pol` and verify both sub-bands use the same polarisation.
- **Cause 2:** Tropospheric delay at the sub-band scale is being confused with ionospheric phase.
- **Fix:** Check for spatial correlation with GACOS or ERA-5 tropospheric delay estimates.

### No ionospheric phase detected even in L-band data

- **Cause:** Mid-latitude night-time acquisitions with TEC < 2 TECU genuinely produce negligible ionospheric phase (< 0.5 rad). Check the ΔTEC estimate — if it is uniformly near zero, the ionosphere is quiet.
- **Action:** This is expected behaviour; no correction is needed.

### GIM correction provides no improvement over uncorrected

- **Cause:** GIM spatial resolution (2.5° × 5°) is too coarse to capture small-scale TEC variations (< 500 km). GIM is ineffective for ionospheric gradients caused by plasma irregularities.
- **Fix:** Use split-spectrum method if coherence permits; consider masking scintillation-affected regions.

### Faraday rotation artifacts in polarimetric data

- **Cause:** L-band quad-pol data acquired at high latitudes or during disturbed geomagnetic conditions (Kp > 4).
- **Fix:** Estimate and correct Faraday rotation using the CPR method (Section 9.3) before computing the ionospheric correction. ISCE3 provides `isce3.polsar.faraday_rotation` for this purpose.
